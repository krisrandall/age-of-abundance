## The structure of a declaration

A declaration is written the way you'd write a clear brief — or a system prompt. It works from the
top down:

1. **The world we want.** At the very top: what kind of world are we trying to create? This is the
   overarching context everything else serves.
2. **The kind of government we want.** Given that world, what is government *for*?
3. **My priorities.** The concrete things you believe government should secure. For the author, these
   begin with: basic needs absolutely taken care of for every citizen — secure housing, enough food,
   access to education and health care approaching free.
4. **How I see the role of government.** A statement of principle. For the author: government's first
   duty is to the people who most need help — those who would otherwise miss out — not to those
   already thriving.

From there it can branch into as much detail as you like — positions on the environment, the economy,
and so on — each stated openly.

## The mechanism that gives it teeth

Once the declaration is public, it becomes a measuring stick:

- A piece of legislation comes through.
- Anyone can compare that bill against the declaration — *using their own AI*, independently — and ask
  whether supporting it furthers the stated goals or contradicts them.
- When a representative votes, you can see whether they are being **true to what they said** they
  stand for.

This pushes transparency *into* government. Politicians who publish a declaration can no longer say one
thing and quietly vote another, because the comparison is open to everyone and easy to run.

## Facts versus narrative — and the bridge between them

This is the deeper point. We tend to **conflate facts with narrative reality** — to treat the stories
we believe as though they were as solid as written law.

- A **bill** is factual: it is written down, it becomes law, it has legal and legislative meaning.
- A **value or ideology** is narrative: real and important, but a reality we *create*, not a fact in
  the same way.

Writing down *"this is what I stand for, these are the policies I think matter"* bridges the two. It
takes your narrative and pins it next to the factual record — the bills you actually support — so the
two can be held against each other. That is how you tell whether a stated philosophy is being lived or
just performed.

## The shift it creates

The goal isn't gotcha politics. It's to change the *level* of the conversation. When everyone's
declaration is public and every vote is checkable against it, talking points stop working. We're left
debating the things that actually matter:

> What kind of world are we trying to create — and does this law take us toward it?

That is a debate worth having. Open Government is simply the structure that makes it possible.
