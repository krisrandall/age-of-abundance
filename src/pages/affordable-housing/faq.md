## Is this a get-rich-quick scheme?

No — the opposite. It's a **slow, solid, long-term** return; something concrete to hold for years. The
rough figuring on the original example was around a **5%** annual yield from rent. The appeal is
stability and security, not a quick flip.

## Will the unit price go up, or stay the same?

It's simply **market rate** — whatever a willing buyer will pay. In practice that broadly tracks the
property's value, so as the property appreciates, units tend to as well. There's no fixed price; the
only special rule is that **existing unit-holders get preference** to buy.

## Why exactly 1,000 units? Can we issue more later?

The figure of 1,000 is borrowed from BrickX (a platform doing the same thing) — it keeps units cheap
enough that almost anyone can buy in, while still allowing larger stakes. **No new units are ever
minted.** Adding units would dilute everyone and force messy arguments about current value. If someone
wants in, they **buy existing units** from a willing seller. Always exactly 1,000, attached to the
property.

## Is my pledge binding?

No. The initial spreadsheet of interest is **an expression of interest only** — to gauge the total
budget. If your circumstances change between pledging and the money actually going in, that's
completely fine. Nothing is committed until entities are formed and funds are deposited.

## How hands-on does the organiser have to be?

For the **first** property, the organiser is happy to take on the setup — working through the
structure with the accountant. After that, the ongoing work (bookkeeping, tax returns, administration)
becomes a **board responsibility**, and much of it can be outsourced. The aim for most investors is
genuinely **set-and-forget**.

## Who actually manages the property?

**One designated person**, so tenants and tradespeople deal with a single contact. That person can be
paid for it — around **8%** of rent, the standard property-manager rate — as an additional income for
taking on the responsibility.

## What if more people want in, or we want a second property?

New people buy **existing units** (with the other holders' consent). For a second property, you create
a **new standalone company + trust**, valued at market when it's set up. Longer term, an umbrella
not-for-profit could replicate the formula across many properties — see [the pathway](../pathway/).

## What stops decisions from stalling if people disappear?

Votes use a **quorum and a time window** — e.g. 30 days, with everyone who participates counting and
non-participants not blocking the outcome. The standard trust deed sets the quorum, and the group can
adjust it by agreement. Fundamental terms are protected; everything else can move.

## Will the pledge spreadsheet be public?

That's **opt-in.** Among a group of co-owners, holdings are effectively shared information anyway — but
anyone who'd rather email their figure privately and be left off a shared sheet can do exactly that.

## How is my personal wealth protected?

The property is owned via a **trust run by a company trustee**. If anything goes wrong, the company —
not your personal assets — is what's exposed (barring director negligence). There's no debt, so there's
no mortgage to default on. See [Governance &amp; legal](../governance-legal/) for the full picture.

## What does it cost to set up and run?

Indicatively: company setup ~$1,200, trust setup ~$600, ongoing ASIC fee ~$260/year, and no ongoing
cost for the unit trust. These establishment costs are folded into the property purchase.
