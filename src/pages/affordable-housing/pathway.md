## Start with one — deliberately

The whole approach is to take a **single, simple first step**: collectively buy *one* property that
gives a solid return, with no grander scheme attached. Prove the mechanism works, then consider doing
it again.

Location matters for that "safe bet." The original example was **Kilcoy** — home to a meat works, so
residential property there rents reliably and easily. **Maleny** has perhaps the strongest rental
security around, but prices are much higher. The point isn't the specific town; it's choosing
somewhere rental income is dependable.

## The process, step by step

1. **Gather expressions of interest.** Find out who's keen and roughly how much they'd put in. This is
   **non-binding** — it's just to see what budget the group is working with. Circumstances can change
   between a pledge and the day money is deposited, and that's fine.
2. **Size the property to the budget.** The total pledged shapes what you buy:
   - A modest total → a single, affordable property (e.g. around $400–500k).
   - A large total (say **$1M**) → either look for something cheaper to keep a **buffer** for changed
     circumstances, or **spread the risk across two properties**. Lower-value properties also tend to
     be easier to sell later, and carry less risk than a single expensive one.
3. **Establish the entities.** Set up the company and unit trust (see
   [Governance &amp; legal](../governance-legal/)), with the establishment costs folded into the
   overall purchase.
4. **Pool the money.** Investments go into the trust's bank account.
5. **Buy the property.** With the entity established and the funds sitting ready, purchase the home.

A **buyer's agent** can source a property to the group's specification — typically around a **2% fee
plus a small upfront amount**, which gets incorporated into the purchase price. (One was on the
original call and was happy to help.)

## How it scales

The first property is a self-contained **company + unit trust**. To grow, you don't complicate that
first one — you repeat the formula:

- **Each new property is its own standalone company + trust**, valued at market when it's set up so
  its units are priced fairly against what's actually being bought.
- Over the top of them all, you could create an **umbrella not-for-profit** carrying the mission and
  vision — *making property investment affordable for anyone*, toward *affordable housing for
  everyone* — that facilitates the same formula again and again. It's a little like a building-society
  structure.

That bigger vision is real and exciting — a collective that, over time, holds and provides genuinely
affordable housing. But the discipline is to **get one off the ground first**, keep it simple, and let
it prove itself.
