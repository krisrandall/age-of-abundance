## The structure: a company and a unit trust

The whole thing rests on two well-established entities working together:

- A **unit trust** — this *holds the property* and *derives the rental income*. Investors are issued
  **units**, much like shares, and each unit-holder has a fixed entitlement to the income and assets
  of the trust in proportion to their holding.
- A **company** — this acts as **trustee** of the trust. A trust isn't a separate legal person, so it
  needs a trustee to act for it. Using a company (rather than individuals) protects everyone's
  personal assets — more on that in [Governance &amp; legal](../governance-legal/).

This was the accountant's recommended structure, in his words:

> The ideal structure for this arrangement would be a company and a unit trust. The company simply
> acts as trustee of the trust and the unit trust holds the property and derives the income. Units are
> issued to investors much like shares in a company and each unitholder has a fixed entitlement to the
> income and assets of the unit trust in proportion to their unitholding.

**Why a unit trust and not a company holding the property directly?** Because when a property is
eventually sold, individuals can claim the **50% capital-gains discount** (if it's been held over 12
months) — but a company can't. The trust passes the income and gains through to the unit-holders, who
handle their own tax. (For this project, selling isn't the goal — but the structure is still the right
one. See the tax note in [Governance &amp; legal](../governance-legal/).)

## The units: fixed, and allocated by contribution

Ownership is divided into a **fixed number of units — 1,000** is the suggested figure (borrowed from
[BrickX](https://www.brickx.com), a platform doing essentially the same thing). Units are allocated
strictly by how much each person contributes.

<div class="callout">
  <span class="callout-title">Worked example — illustrative only</span>
  <p>For a <strong>$300,000</strong> property split into 1,000 units, each unit costs <strong>$300</strong>.</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Put in <strong>$60,000</strong> → you hold <strong>200 units</strong> → <strong>20% ownership</strong>.</li>
    <li>Put in <strong>$3,000</strong> → you hold <strong>10 units</strong> → 1% ownership.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>1,000 units keeps it affordable for someone who otherwise couldn't buy in at all, while still
  allowing a larger investor to take a meaningful stake.</p>
</div>

The crucial rule: **the total number of units never changes.** You don't mint new units later —
that would dilute everyone and force awkward arguments about what the property is now worth. If
someone new wants in, they **buy existing units** from a willing seller. Always exactly 1,000,
attached to the property.

## How the money flows

1. **Rent comes in.** Tenants pay into a bank account the **trust** owns.
2. **Costs come out.** Landlord insurance and maintenance are paid from that account.
3. **A kitty is kept.** A baseline buffer (e.g. **$10,000**) stays in the account for surprises.
4. **The surplus is distributed.** Each period — quarterly is the suggested default, though monthly
   (like a real-estate agent's cycle) also works — everything above the kitty is paid out to
   unit-holders **in proportion to their holding.** The cadence and the exact rules are board
decisions — see [Governance &amp; legal](../governance-legal/).

<div class="callout warm">
  <span class="callout-title">Worked numbers — back-of-napkin, illustrative</span>
  <p>The property originally looked at was a ~$300k house in Kilcoy (near the meat works, so reliably
  rentable), let at <strong>$370/week</strong>. Rough figuring: about <strong>$70/week</strong> covers
  landlord's insurance and maintenance, leaving roughly <strong>$300/week</strong> flowing back to
  unit-holders — on the order of a <strong>~5% annual return</strong>.</p>
  <p>These figures are a few years old and the example property has long since sold — treat them purely
  as an illustration of <em>how the model works</em>, not as current numbers.</p>
</div>

## What it feels like to be in it

For most unit-holders this is deliberately **set-and-forget**: you allocate money you can afford to
invest, you put it in, and you get to forget about it — collecting your share of the rent each period.
If you'd rather be active, you can put your hand up for a paid role (managing the property, or running
the distributions) and earn an additional income for it. Both are covered in
[Governance &amp; legal](../governance-legal/).
