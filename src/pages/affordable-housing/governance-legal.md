Almost none of this is invented. The structures are standard templates that accountants and property
lawyers use every day. The notes below come from a long conversation with an accountant who sets up
exactly these arrangements.

## Who decides what

The **board** is simply the **directors of the trustee company**. Unit-holders will naturally want a
say in the day-to-day decisions the company makes on the trust's behalf — so in practice, people who
invest and acquire units typically also become directors.

The board handles the ongoing decisions: keeping the property rented, distributions, maintenance, and
the big calls like whether to ever sell. Routine decisions can be made at a regular (say quarterly)
meeting; significant ones go to a vote of unit-holders.

**Voting that doesn't stall.** Over many years, people drift, move, and forget about an investment.
So decisions use a quorum and a time window — for example: *a change is proposed; there are 30 days to
vote; everyone who participates counts, everyone who doesn't, doesn't.* The standard trust deed already
sets a quorum percentage, and the group can amend it by agreement. Some things are fundamental and
can't be changed; everything else can move without being held hostage by an unreachable member.

## Paid roles

Some jobs need a reliable owner, and it's appropriate to pay the person who takes them on — so there's
a real incentive for the work to get done well:

| Role | What it covers | Suggested pay |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Property manager** | One designated point of contact for tenants; keeps the place rented and maintained | ~**8%** of rental income (the normal agent rate) |
| **Treasurer** | Runs the distributions and bookkeeping | ~**2%** (illustrative) |
| **Maintenance** | Handling repairs as they arise | by agreement |

Designating **one** person as property manager matters — tenants deal with a single contact, not a
committee. These roles can be formalised (recorded in the company minutes, with agreed remuneration)
or left informal, in which case everyone shares responsibility for someone stepping up.

## Buying and selling units

- **Price is whatever the market will bear.** A unit's value broadly tracks the property's value: you
  look at what the trust holds (essentially the bank account plus the property's real-estate value)
  and price accordingly. The bank balance is known exactly; the property just needs a valuation.
- **Existing holders get first refusal.** Anyone wanting to sell must first offer their units to the
  other unit-holders. This is a standard clause in the trust deed.
- **New holders need consent.** If no existing holder wants the units, the seller can find an outside
  buyer — *but the other unit-holders must approve that new person joining.* This protects everyone
  from waking up to an unwanted business partner. (It's standard, and it's there for good reason —
  the accountant has seen a company partner sell his share without the other's consent, and a stranger
  turn up announcing "I'm your new business partner." With these rules, that can't happen.)
- **Estates.** If a holder dies, their units form part of their estate and pass on under their will.
- **Transfers are a capital-gains event.** Selling or even gifting units is a disposal — and there's a
  *deemed market value* rule, so capital-gains tax can apply **even if no money changes hands.** A
  straight share/unit transfer attracts no stamp duty, but because there's underlying real estate,
  stamp duty *might* still apply — that's one to confirm with the lawyer.

## The entities, in plain English

- A **trust is not a separate legal person.** It can't act on its own, so it's run by a **trustee.**
- The trustee can be individuals or a company. **Best practice is a company** — a "$2 company" — so
  that if anything ever goes wrong, only the company is exposed.
- **Why that protects you:** if the trustee does something it shouldn't and gets sued, the claimant
  generally has to sue the *trustee* first. If the trustee is a group of individuals, all their
  personal assets are on the line. If it's a company with only a few shares, that's the only money at
  risk — unless directors were negligent. The company is a roadblock in front of your personal assets.
- **Two rule-books:** the **trust deed** governs the trust and the unit-holders (distributions,
  transfers, quorum); the **company constitution** governs the company (it's the directors' rule-book).
  Both are standard documents, and both can be amended by agreement and recorded in minutes.
- **Decisions get minuted.** Who manages the property, how they're paid, when distributions happen —
  these are recorded in the minutes of board meetings (which can be held as often as you like, even
  annually).

## The bank account

The **trust owns the account**. To protect the money, use **multiple signatories — at least two
unrelated people.** You can go further (e.g. all four investors as signatories) but the more
signatories, the more cumbersome day-to-day banking becomes. Two unrelated signatories is the usual
sensible minimum.

## Setup and ongoing costs

From the accountant (indicative figures):

| Item | Cost |
| --- | --- |
| Company setup | ~$1,200 |
| Trust setup | ~$600 |
| Ongoing ASIC fee (company) | ~$260 / year |
| Ongoing unit-trust cost | none |

These establishment costs are simply **folded into the purchase** — incorporated into the overall
cost of getting the property, much like the buyer's-agent fee.

## A note on tax — and why a recent change doesn't threaten this

The headline tax facts: if the property is held for **more than 12 months**, a sale qualifies for the
**50% capital-gains discount** — only half the gain is taxable, and that half is added to the
individual's income and taxed at their marginal rate (so a low-income year means a low rate). A
**main-residence exemption** only comes into play if you actually live in the property — in which case
a unit trust may *not* be the right structure, because it gives that exemption away.

For anything property-specific — like whether stamp duty applies to a unit transfer — the property
**lawyer** is the right person to confirm the detail.
